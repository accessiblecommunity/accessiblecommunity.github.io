## Introduction

Accessibility can feel overwhelming at first, but getting started is easier than you think! The key is to approach it with curiosity and a willingness to make small, meaningful changes. Accessibility isn't about giving anyone an "unfair advantage," it is more about making spaces, products, and services usable for everyone.

Here are a few ways you can begin:

1. **Think inclusively from the start**. When designing a website, creating documents, or building a space, ask yourself: "Who might be excluded?" Then, take simple steps to remove those barriers. Accessible Communities provides a FREE evaluation tool to help you evaluate your accessibility.
2. **Use assistive technology as a guide**. Tools like screen readers, captioning, voice recognition, or alternative input devices can show you what users experience.  Check out the [video introduction to web accessibility](https://www.w3.org/WAI/videos/standards-and-benefits/) from the Web Accessibility Initiate to learn more about assistive technology.
3. **Start small and keep learning**.
	This is a step that is underutilized. Focus on one area first, such as making your PDFs readable or adding captions to videos, then you can expand on other forms of accessibility from there. Accessibility is an ongoing process, therefore, allow yourself some grace and continue to educate yourself and those around you.

   Would you like to receive a simple but effective accessibility tip in your inbox each week? Sign up for our [Tip of the Week](/tips/).
4. **Ask for feedback**. Connect with disabled users or colleagues and listen to their experiences. Their insight is invaluable and often points out things you may have never considered.
5. **Challenge assumptions, look beyond stigma**. Question the stereotypes you might have been told or have heard about disabilities and accessibility. Look past stigma and see the people underneath.  Subscribe to our Day in the Life podcast to learn more about living with disabilities from the people who do so.

## Conclusion

Remember that accessibility benefits everyone, not just the disabled community. Clearer communication, easier navigation, and thoughtful design will end up helping everyone. By starting small and staying curious, you can create a more inclusive environment in any workplace, classroom, or digital space\!

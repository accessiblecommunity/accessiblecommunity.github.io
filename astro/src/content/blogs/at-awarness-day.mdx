## Why This Day Is Personal to Me

Assistive technology gave me back my voice—literally.

I became quadriplegic at age 10. From that moment on, I learned that independence does not just happen. It depends on having the right tools, support, and information at the right time.

Assistive technology has been a big part of my journey. It also led me to the work I do today.

Through Esther’s Walking Journey, I work as an accessibility writer, AT user tester, speaker, and advocate. I partner with product teams, educators, families, and individuals to solve real-life accessibility problems. I am deeply grateful for every part of this path.

April 22 is National Assistive Technology Awareness Day. This day helps more people understand what assistive technology can make possible. It also honors the people who use these tools every day, the professionals who support them, and the communities working to improve access.

For many people, assistive technology is the difference between being left out and being able to fully take part in life.

## What Is Assistive Technology?

Assistive technology, often called AT, means tools that help disabled people communicate, learn, work, move, and live with more independence.

In simple terms, assistive technology helps remove barriers. It helps people do what they need or want to do.

Here are some common examples:

* **AAC devices** help people communicate when speaking is hard or not possible. AAC stands for augmentative and alternative communication. It can include speech devices, apps, communication boards, and other ways to share thoughts, needs, and ideas.
* **Screen readers** read digital text out loud. They help people who are blind or have low vision use computers and phones.
* **Power wheelchairs** help people with limited mobility move more independently.
* **Alternative keyboards** make typing easier for people who cannot use a standard keyboard.
* **Head tracking systems** let someone control a computer by moving their head.
* **Captions** show spoken words as text. They make videos, meetings, and events easier to follow.
* **Text-to-speech tools** read written words out loud.
* **Smart home tools** help people control lights, doors, phones, and appliances on their own.

Assistive technology is not “extra help” or a luxury. For many people—including me—it is part of daily life.

## Why Assistive Technology Matters

Assistive technology is more than a device. It creates access.

Think of AT as a bridge. The goal is not to change the person. The goal is to make a task easier to do. That task might be writing an email, going to school, joining a meeting, making a phone call, or controlling something at home.

Access can affect almost every part of life:

* **Communication:** sharing ideas, asking questions, and making choices
* **Education:** taking part in school and learning with classmates
* **Employment:** doing job tasks and contributing at work
* **Independence:** doing things on your own terms
* **Privacy:** making private choices without needing someone else to help
* **Social connection:** staying in touch with people who matter

I rely on AT every day. The tools I use include switch control, voice access, speech-to-text, keyboard shortcuts, and built-in accessibility settings. Each tool helps me in a different way.

Together, these tools help me communicate, work, and move through my day. They help me be productive and independent.

AAC is how I share my ideas. It is how I take part in conversations and do my work. When I think about independence, assistive technology is part of every piece of that picture.

## What Access Looks Like in Real Life

The value of assistive technology is not just an idea. It shows up in everyday moments.

A student uses text-to-speech, and school becomes easier to manage.

A nonspeaking person uses AAC to share ideas, ask questions, and make choices.

A worker uses voice input or adaptive equipment to complete job tasks.

An older adult uses reminder tools to stay safe and connected.

A person uses captions to follow a meeting, video, class, or event.

These are not rare or dramatic stories. They are quiet, daily examples of how access changes what is possible.

## The Awareness Gap—and Why It Matters

National Assistive Technology Awareness Day also matters because many people still do not know what assistive technology is available.

When people do not know about AT, it can affect their daily lives. It can affect communication, school, work, safety, independence, and social connection.

Access should never depend on luck—but too often, it does.

Some people may qualify for AT but never learn that a helpful tool exists. Others may receive a device but not get enough training to use it well. Teachers, employers, and families may want to help but may not know where to start.

Some common barriers include:

* **Limited awareness:** people have not heard of tools that could help
* **Funding challenges:** cost and insurance can be hard to figure out
* **Lack of training:** getting a device without learning how to use it is not true access
* **Confusing systems:** finding support can feel overwhelming

For many people, the problem is not a lack of effort. It is a lack of information, funding, and guidance.

Awareness matters. But awareness alone is not enough. It must lead to action.

## What Needs to Change

Improving access to assistive technology takes more than one person. It takes schools, workplaces, organizations, designers, and communities working together.

Every part of the community has a role to play.

**Systems and organizations can:**

* Make AT easier to find
* Make funding easier to understand
* Build accessibility into policies and services from the start
* Listen to disabled people and AT users when making decisions

**Educators and employers can:**

* Learn about common AT tools
* Provide training and support—not just the device
* Create classrooms and workplaces where more people can take part
* Ask people what they need instead of assuming

**Designers and content creators can:**

* Add captions to videos
* Make websites work with keyboards and screen readers
* Use clear headings and easy-to-read formatting
* Build accessibility in from the beginning, not as an afterthought

Accessibility often helps more people than we realize. Captions help deaf and hard-of-hearing people. They also help people in noisy places, people learning a language, and people who understand information better by reading.

Small changes can open doors for many people.

## The Role of Community

Community is one of the most powerful parts of improving access.

Advocates, educators, AT specialists, disabled people, families, and organizations all help connect people with tools and information.

When people share what works, others can find solutions faster. This can save time, lower stress, and reduce the trial-and-error process of figuring it out alone.

Shared knowledge helps turn awareness into real access.

## What You Can Do

Everyone has a role in making assistive technology easier to access. You do not have to change a whole system to make a difference. You can start with one small step:

* Learn about one AT tool you had not heard of before
* Share a resource with someone who may benefit
* Add captions to your videos
* Check that your website or content works with a keyboard
* Ask yourself: “Who might be left out by what I’m creating?”
* Listen when disabled people and AT users explain what they need

Assistive technology is both a lifeline and a right. It helps people communicate, take part, work, learn, and live with more independence. I feel that deeply every single day.

## Key Takeaways

* **Assistive technology removes barriers** to what a person needs or wants to do.
* **AT includes many different tools,** such as AAC devices, screen readers, power wheelchairs, captions, smart home tools, and more.
* **Access shapes daily life.** Communication, school, work, privacy, and independence all depend on it.
* **Many people still do not know what tools exist.** Awareness is the first step toward change.
* **Awareness must lead to action.** Better funding, training, design, and support are all needed.
* **Everyone can help.** Small, steady steps from people, organizations, and communities add up.

Access is not optional. It shapes daily life.

National Assistive Technology Awareness Day reminds us that the right tools can open doors, restore independence, and help people fully take part in their communities.

That means everything to me—and it can mean everything to someone you know.

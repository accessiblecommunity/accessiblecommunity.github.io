## Introduction

Education is often talked about like it's equal for everyone. Same classrooms, same expectations, same path forward. But for
a lot of students, that idea doesn't match reality at all.

From elementary school to higher education, students with disabilities are constantly navigating systems that weren't built with them in mind. Sometimes it shows up in obvious ways, like buildings without ramps, broken elevators (or none), or even classrooms that are physically hard to access. Other times, it's quieter but just as limiting: lectures delivered too fast to follow, materials that aren't available in accessible or multiple formats, and support systems that take too long to respond, if they respond at all.

What tends to get missed a lot is that accessibility isn't about physical spaces only, it is also about how learning actually happens. This means how students access information, participate fully, ask for help without barriers, and exist in an environment where they aren't constantly having to justify their needs. Research consistently shows that these barriers are not rare or isolated, they are built into systems that were not designed for diverse learning needs in the first place. And when education isn't designed for everyone from the start, students are often left adapting to the system instead of the system adapting to them.

In this blog, we're going to take a look at what barriers look like in real educational settings, how they affect students, and what ways accessibility could actually look like when it's treated as a foundation rather than an afterthought.

## The Barriers Most Don't See

### Sensory, Environmental, and Physical Barriers:

Accessibility in education doesn't just come down to whether a building has a ramp or elevator. The environment itself can create barriers that make learning harder before a student even reaches the classroom. Poor lighting, loud or crowded spaces, inconsistent building layouts, and inaccessible pathways all contribute to the overall learning environment, which can either support productivity when thoughtfully designed or become overwhelming and difficult to navigate when it is not. For some students, sensory factors such as noise, visual clutter, or lack of quiet spaces can make it difficult to focus or fully participate. Even temperature, seating arrangements, or overcrowded hallways can affect how accessible a school environment actually feels in day-to-day life.

### Learning and Communication Barriers:

Beyond the physical environment, the way education is delivered can also create barriers. Fast-paced lectures, unclear instructions, inaccessible course materials, and limited ways to participate can make it difficult for students to fully engage with content.

Communication plays a major role here too. When expectations are not clearly explained or when students have difficulty communicating their needs, it becomes harder to access support systems or request adjustments. In many cases, students are left trying to interpret requirements or advocate for themselves without consistent guidance.

### Social and Emotional Barriers:

Some of the most overlooked barriers in education are social rather than structural. Students with disabilities may face stigma, misunderstanding, bullying, or social exclusion from their peers or even within institutional spaces. This typically leads to isolation, difficulty forming connections, or feeling like they have to hide their needs in order to avoid being treated differently.

There is also the emotional burden that comes with constantly navigating systems that were not designed with accessibility in mind. Many students find themselves needing to repeatedly disclose personal information, justify accommodations, or push through environments that feel unwelcoming. Over time, this impacts confidence, well-being, and an overall sense of belonging in educational spaces.

## Why Do So Many Students Struggle Silently?

	A lot of students with disabilities don't openly talk about the challenges they're facing in educational settings. One major reason for this is stigma. There is still a persistent fear of being treated differently, underestimated, or labeled in ways that affect how others perceive their ability. Because of this, many students choose to downplay their needs rather than risk being misunderstood or dismissed.

	Disclosure also seems to play a big role in this silence. Before even getting into what that process looks like, it's worth asking a simple question: how would it feel to constantly have to explain your health, justify your needs, and repeatedly prove that those needs are real? For many students, that is the reality of navigating education with a disability. It can mean explaining personal medical details to teachers or administrators, repeating the same information across different systems, and sometimes even feeling like you have to defend your own experiences just to access support. On top of that, there's often the fear of not being believed, being labeled as exaggerating, or being seen as less capable because of what you disclose, For some, this might lead to holding back information altogether–not because they don't need support, but because the process of asking for it feels exposing, exhausting, and even discouraging.

## From Barriers to Better Design

	One of the biggest issues in education is that accessibility is often reactive instead of built into the system from the start. Support tends to appear only after a student has already run into barriers, rather than being embedded into how learning environments are designed in the first place. This can show up in delayed accommodations, where students wait weeks or even longer for adjustments that are essential for them to fully participate in their education. By the time support is in place, students might have already fallen behind or had to navigate through unnecessary stress just to access basic course requirements.

	Institutional systems also play a large role in these barriers. Many schools and universities still rely on processes that are not designed with accessibility in mind. Accommodation requests can be complicated, inconsistent, or heavily dependent on individual departments, which creates uneven experiences for students. In some cases, there is also a lack of training among staff and educators, meaning that even when policies exist, they're not always understood or implemented effectively. Accessibility becomes something that exists on paper, but not always in practice.

	Inaccessible systems add another layer to this problem. Digital platforms, course materials, and communication systems are not always designed to be fully usable with assistive technology or alternative learning needs. When these systems are difficult to use, students are often left to figure out workarounds on their own, which shifts the burden of accessibility away from institutions and onto the students themselves.

	This is where the conversation needs to shift. Instead of treating accessibility as something that is added after the fact, education systems need to move toward designing learning environments that are flexible from the beginning for all types of students. This is where approaches like Universal Design for Learning (UDL) become important because it is a practical framework that prioritizes multiple ways of engaging with content, participating in learning, and demonstrating understanding. In more accessible learning environments, flexibility is not an exception, it is the standard.

UDL provides three principles: provide multiple ways of **engagement**, provide multiple ways of **representation**, and provide multiple ways of **action and expression**. Multiple means of engagement is the “why” of learning. This principle encourages offering different materials, teaching methods, and opportunities for participation that align with varying student motivations, interests, and goals. When students are able to connect with learning in ways that are engaging and accessible to them, they are more likely to remain motivated and meaningfully absorb the material being taught.

Multiple means of representation focuses on the “what” of learning. This means recognizing that students process and understand information differently, so content needs to be presented in a variety of formats that support different learning needs and improve access to knowledge.

Lastly, multiple means of action and expression focuses on the “how” of learning. This principle encourages providing students with different ways to demonstrate what they know, whether through writing, speaking, physical actions, creative projects, music, or other forms of communication and expression.

Rather than functioning as a one-size-fits-all model, UDL is designed to create flexibility across educational environments and can be applied in online synchronous or asynchronous settings, hybrid learning environments, and traditional in-person classrooms.

An example of this approach in practice could look like a history teacher covering the same lesson through multiple formats instead of relying only on a lecture and written notes. Students might be given the option to watch a short video, listen to audio content, read written material, or participate in a group discussion depending on what helps them absorb information most effectively. Instead of requiring every student to complete the same type of assignment, the teacher could allow students to demonstrate their understanding through a written paper, presentation, visual project, recorded response, or another approved format. Small adjustments can also make a meaningful difference. Posting schedules and expectations clearly online, providing captions on videos, sharing materials before class, or allowing flexibility in how students participate can help foster a learning environment that is easier to access and navigate. While these changes may seem simple, they can significantly reduce barriers and create more inclusive educational experiences for a wide range of students.

## Where We Can Start

While larger systemic changes are important, smaller practical adjustments can also significantly improve accessibility within educational settings:

* Provide captions, transcripts, and accessible digital materials for online content and lectures.
* Post schedules, assignments, and expectations, clearly in accessible locations.
* Offer multiple ways for students to participate, communicate, and demonstrate understanding.
* Share class materials before lectures when possible to allow students time to prepare and process information.
* Design online learning platforms with accessibility in mind, including compatibility with assistive technology.
* Create quieter or lower-stimulation spaces for students who may become easily overwhelmed in crowded environments.
* Train educators and staff on accessibility, accommodations, and inclusive communication practices.
* Allow flexibility when appropriate with participation methods, deadlines, or assignment formats.
* Encourage open communication so students feel safer discussing support needs without fear of judgement.
* Ask students directly what supports or adjustments they need to help them learn most effectively.

## Conclusion

Accessibility in education is about creating learning environments where students are able to fully participate, engage, and succeed without constantly having to fight for access along the way. When we make inclusivity and accessibility the foundation of our educational system rather than an afterthought, we open the door for more students to fully engage and thrive with learning. True accessibility is not about treating everyone the same, but about recognizing that students learn, communicate, and engage with education in different ways. Young people are naturally inquisitive and capable in many different ways, even when traditional systems fail to recognize it. When students are supported with accessible environments and flexible opportunities to learn, we often see just how much potential was there all along.
